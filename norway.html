Norway: 5-7 Years Ahead of the UK

Norway leads the world in EV adoption thanks to generous tax exemptions, cheap hydroelectric power, and toll/parking incentives since the 1990s. While the UK may not match Norway's speed, the direction of travel is clear. What's happening to their petrol stations is a preview of the UK's future.

2016

Peak fuel sales
5.26 billion litres of petrol and diesel sold - the highest ever. EV market share: 29%.

2018

Tipping point
EVs reach 49% of new car sales. Fuel sales begin noticeable decline.

2020

Majority electric
75% of new cars are electric. Total petroleum sales down to 8.2B litres. Circle K announces charger rollout.

2022

Station transformation
"Bensinstasjon" becomes "energistasjon". Major chains begin replacing pumps with fast chargers.

2024

Diesel cars overtaken
EVs now 29% of total fleet - more than petrol or diesel cars. 3,500 station jobs lost since 2014. Staffed stations down to 900.

2025

Mission accomplished
96% of new cars are electric. Circle K operates 500+ high-speed chargers at forecourts. Remaining stations increasingly serve commercial vehicles and tourists.

Key Insight: The Lag Effect

Norway peaked in new diesel sales around the same time as the UK (2016), but their EV adoption has been much faster. The UK is following the same trajectory but ~5-7 years behind.

When the UK reaches Norway's current 29% fleet EV share (projected ~2032-2035), we can expect similar impacts on fuel infrastructure.

Mapping Norway's Experience to the UK

Methodology: We align the UK's diesel car timeline with Norway's based on the peak diesel sales year (both 2016). Norway's fuel decline appears gentler because it includes all petroleum (petrol, diesel, and commercial). The UK projection focuses specifically on diesel cars only, which shows a steeper decline because: (1) diesel car sales collapsed faster than petrol, (2) the UK diesel fleet is older and scrappage is accelerating, and (3) diesels are being driven less each year.

Diesel pumps disappear from urban forecourts; "energy stations" become norm

What Norway Teaches Us

  • Station transformation happens faster than expected
  • Rural areas feel the impact first and hardest
  • Commercial vehicles (vans, trucks) sustain diesel longer
  • Chargers can share space with pumps during transition
  • The "energy station" model can work economically

Key Differences for the UK

  • UK has 10x more vehicles - larger absolute numbers
  • UK diesel peaked at 47% of new sales (higher dependency)
  • UK EV charging infrastructure is less mature
  • UK has more commercial diesel vehicles (vans)
  • UK adoption may be faster once tipping point reached

The Bottom Line

Norway's experience confirms our diesel consumption model. When EV share reaches 50%+ of new sales (UK expected ~2028), fuel sales begin accelerating decline. When EVs reach 30% of total fleet (UK expected ~2032-2035), stations begin significant restructuring.

The question isn't if, but when.
