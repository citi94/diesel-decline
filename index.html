The Problem

In 2016, nearly half of all new cars sold in the UK were diesel. Today, that figure has collapsed to just 5%.

The diesel fleet is ageing rapidly. With so few new diesels entering the market, the total number of diesel vehicles on UK roads is shrinking every year.

This raises a critical question: when will diesel demand fall so low that petrol stations stop selling it?

Why Does This Matter?

  • Millions of people still drive diesel cars
  • Rural areas depend on diesel vehicles
  • Commercial vehicles are 85% diesel
  • Infrastructure changes take years to plan

Key Findings

1. New Diesel Sales Have Collapsed

Diesel's share of new car sales has fallen from 47% in 2016 to just 5% in 2025 - an unprecedented collapse driven by Dieselgate, emissions regulations, and the rise of electric vehicles.

2. The Diesel Fleet is Ageing Fast

Most diesel cars on UK roads today were registered between 2013-2017. These vehicles are now 8-12 years old, and there are very few younger diesels to replace them.

3. Diesel Vehicles Don't Last Forever

Using MOT data, we calculated survival rates. By age 15, only 66% of diesel cars are still on the road. By age 20, just 18% remain.

4. Diesels Are Being Driven Less

Not only are there fewer diesels, but they're being driven less. A 5-year-old diesel in 2015 had 60,700 miles on average. Today, it's just 50,400 miles - a 17% drop.

This reflects changing work patterns (more working from home), higher fuel costs, and owners switching high-mileage driving to newer vehicles.

What Does This Mean?

By 2030

Diesel demand will be 40% lower than today. Some rural stations may start removing diesel pumps.

By 2035

With 74% less demand, many stations will question whether diesel is economically viable.

By 2040

Diesel cars will be rare collector items. Commercial vehicles will be the main diesel users.

Methodology

1

Vehicle Survival Rates

We analysed 26 million diesel vehicles to calculate what percentage are still on the road at each age. Vehicles "survive" if they have a valid MOT in 2024-2025.

2

Annual Mileage by Age

By comparing consecutive MOT odometer readings, we calculated how many miles diesel vehicles drive each year, broken down by vehicle age.

3

Fleet Projection

Starting with historical sales data, we applied survival rates to project how many diesels will be on the road each year through 2040.

4

Consumption Calculation

Fleet size √ó annual mileage √ó fuel economy = total litres consumed. We validated this against actual fuel sales data.

Model Validation

We backtested our model against historical data from 2015-2024. The model predictions were within 10-15% of actual fuel consumption figures - a reasonable margin given the complexity of the system.

Limitations & Caveats

  • This analysis covers diesel cars only, not vans, lorries, or buses
  • Commercial vehicles (vans/HGVs) will maintain diesel demand longer than cars
  • Regional variation exists - rural areas may retain diesel infrastructure longer
  • Policy changes (e.g., ULEZ expansion) could accelerate or slow the decline
  • Some external data (pre-2019 fleet sizes) are estimates, not confirmed figures
